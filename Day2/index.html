Service Strategy (big picture)

Service Strategy decides which services IT should offer, who they are for, and how they support business goals.

Plain language idea

Instead of buying tools randomly, Service Strategy asks:

  • What problem are we solving?
  • Who will use this service?
  • How does it create business value?
  • Is this the best place to spend money and time?
Scenario: A logistics company has shipment delays because tracking is manual and inconsistent. Strategy answer: propose a real‑time tracking service that cuts delays and complaints.

Key components

Service portfolio Demand management Financial management Business outcomes

Service portfolio: list of all services: new, live and retired.

Demand management: predicts and shapes how much each service will be used.

Financial management: makes sure services are affordable and justified.

Business outcomes: clear results like fewer outages, faster processes, happier customers.

Ask yourself: “If I were CIO, would I approve this service?” Strategy is about saying “no” as well as “yes”.

Service Design (turn idea into reality)

Service Design takes the strategy and produces a complete, practical design so the service works well from day one.

Five aspects of design

  • Service solutions: what the service does end‑to‑end and how it behaves.
  • Tools & systems: monitoring, ticketing, CMDB, knowledge base, portals.
  • Technology & architecture: cloud vs on‑prem, security, integrations.
  • Processes: incident, change, request, backup, access, etc.
  • Measurement: KPIs, SLAs, availability, response time, satisfaction.

All of this is captured in a Service Design Package (SDP) that transition and operations can follow.

Simple design scenario

New secure self‑service password reset portal:

  • Use MFA and integrate with Active Directory / Azure AD.
  • Design a clear, mobile‑friendly interface for global users.
  • Plan 24/7 availability and monitoring.
  • Train service desk for exceptions.
  • Track KPIs like success rate and reduction in password tickets.
Outcome: thousands of routine calls disappear, users fix issues themselves.
Good design reduces firefighting after go‑live. Design once, benefit for years.

Portfolio & Demand

Service Portfolio Management chooses which services live in your environment. Demand Management makes sure they are used efficiently.

Service Portfolio Management

Manages every service across its life: from idea, to live, to retired.

  • Pipeline: ideas and services being designed or built.
  • Service Catalogue: all live, customer‑facing services.
  • Retired services: switched‑off but kept for history and audit.

Typical steps: define a proposal, analyse value/cost/risk, approve or reject, then create a Service Charter to move to design.

Demand Management

Makes sure capacity roughly matches use – not too much, not too little.

  • Patterns of Business Activity: e.g. payroll spikes at month‑end, retail spikes on weekends.
  • User profiles: groups with similar usage (developers, sales, finance, etc.).
  • Techniques: trend analysis, workload profiling, scheduling, differential charging.
Example: e‑commerce traffic surges on Black Friday, so capacity is scaled up in advance instead of systems crashing.
Portfolio asks “Should this service exist?” Demand asks “How much will they use it?”

Financial Management for IT Services

Financial Management makes IT costs clear, controlled and linked to value.

Three core activities

  • Budgeting: plan how much money IT will need (hardware, licences, cloud, people, training).
  • Accounting: track actual spending by service or department, including total cost of ownership (TCO).
  • Charging: decide who pays for what – or at least show them their costs (“showback”).

The goal is not just “cut costs”, but “spend in the right places and prove value”.

Helpful concepts

  • CapEx vs OpEx: big one‑off purchases vs ongoing subscriptions.
  • ROI: are the benefits of this service greater than its full cost over time?
  • Cost transparency: business leaders see where IT money actually goes.
Scenario: Before funding a new mobile app, the organisation compares cost, expected benefits and alternatives before saying “yes”.
Financials turn “IT is expensive” into clear numbers and choices. Money talks – in ITIL too.
